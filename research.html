Bio

I am an applied microeconomist with interests in (i) political economy, where I study how political institutions and incentives shape policy design and implementation; (ii) development and culture, where I study how informal institutions and norms shape behavior and the effectiveness of policy in developing-country settings; and (iii) gender, where I study the determinants of gender gaps in household decisions, and labor-market trajectories. Across projects, I combine experimental and observational methods, including administrative data, text-as-data, and machine-learning tools. I currently work on projects in India, Denmark, and the United Kingdom.

I will join Princeton University as a postdoctoral researcher in September 2026.

Working Papers

Political Empowerment and Marital Practices

R&R, Journal of Development Economics

Abstract

This paper studies whether expanding women’s political representation changes marital practices in rural India. Using the reservation of village council leadership positions for women under India’s 1992 constitutional amendment, I show that exposure to female leaders delays marriage by improving women’s economic opportunities, but also generates a backlash in the form of higher dowry payments. This backlash is concentrated in more patriarchal settings, where social penalties for delayed marriage are strongest. The findings show that political empowerment can shift marital norms, but that its effects are constrained and partly offset by existing social norms.

Government by Discussion: Deliberation, Media, and Policy-Making

with Elaheh Fatemipour

Abstract

This paper studies whether parliamentary deliberation shapes policy-making, and how its effects depend on public exposure. Using quasi-random variation from the computerized ballot that determines which questions are discussed during Question Hour in the Indian Parliament, we show that debated issues are more likely to appear in subsequent bills and that related bills move more quickly through the legislative process. We further show that these effects are substantially amplified by media coverage: reductions in news attention weaken the policy impact of debate by half, implying that public exposure accounts for an important share of deliberation’s effect.

The Cultural Premium: How Cultural Distance, Economic Contributions and Value Alignment Shape Immigration Attitudes

with Elaheh Fatemipour

Abstract

We measure a cultural premium in immigration attitudes: the additional income a culturally distant-origin immigrant must earn to be equally preferred to a culturally close-origin immigrant to remain in the host country. Using a representative survey experiment in the United Kingdom, we show that this premium exceeds £20,000 when the immigrant’s personal values are unknown, but falls by 73% when respondents learn that his values align with UK norms. Revealing value alignment also reduces concerns about cultural erosion by 35%. The results suggest that natives are willing to trade off substantial economic gains to avoid culturally distant-origin immigrants, and that this premium reflects, to a large extent, signals that natives infer from cultural origin about immigrants’ personal values.

Postpartum Depression and the Motherhood Penalty

with Sonia Bhalotra, N. Meltem Daysal, Louis Fréget, Jonas Cuzulan Hirani, Mircea Trandafir, Miriam Wüst, and Tom Zohar

Abstract

Using Danish administrative data linked to validated postpartum depression screenings, we study how postpartum mental health shocks shape women’s labor market trajectories after childbirth. We show that depressed and non-depressed mothers exhibit similar pre-birth trends, but diverge sharply after birth, with persistent employment gaps that are larger among less educated mothers and those in less family-friendly jobs. The findings identify postpartum depression as an important and unequal contributor to the motherhood penalty.

Publications

Exponential Growth Bias in the Prediction of COVID-19 Spread and Economic Expectation

with Ritwik Banerjee · Economica, 2023

Abstract

Exponential growth bias (EGB) is the tendency of people to perceive a growth process as linear when in fact it is exponential. We study EGB in predictions about the spread of COVID-19 and examine how correcting this bias affects economic expectations and risky investment choices. Using four experimental interventions, we show that simple feedback and forecast information substantially reduce the bias. Correcting the bias also moderates expectations about future macroeconomic conditions and reduces risky investment.

Exponential-Growth Prediction Bias and Compliance with Safety Measures Related to COVID-19

with Ritwik Banerjee and Joydeep Bhattacharya · Social Science & Medicine, 2020

Abstract

We define exponential-growth prediction bias (EGPB) as the systematic error arising from an incorrect prediction of the number of positive COVID cases x-weeks hence when presented with y-weeks of prior, actual data on the same. We study EGPB in beliefs about the spread of COVID-19 and its consequences for compliance with safety measures. Using an incentivized online experiment across countries at different stages of the pandemic, we show that respondents systematically underestimate how quickly infections grow, and that greater bias is associated with lower compliance with protective behaviors. We further show that a simple behavioral nudge that presents prior case data in raw numbers, rather than graphs, significantly reduces this bias. The findings suggest that clearer communication of epidemic growth can improve risk perception and support public-health compliance.
